About the Course

An Extraordinary Intellectual & Literary Legacy

In this course, we investigate the unique situation of German-speaking Jews by concentrating on the extraordinary intellectual and literary legacy of the "German-Jewish dialogue" before and after the Holocaust.

In our close reading of a variety of texts this dialogue has yielded, we pay attention to the role of the German language as this dialogue's medium — and to the ways in which German-Jewish writing challenged its boundaries by attempting what Franz Kafka called an "assault on all frontiers of language."

We explore questions of belonging and difference and how these themes are expressed through the use of the German language by Jewish writers and thinkers. We study some of the greatest practitioners of German writing, from Heine to Kafka to Arendt to Celan, and focus on the way in which these writers reflected on their relation to the German language as European Others.

We further consider various theories of language and translation born of the encounter between Germans and Jews, and conclude with autobiographical accounts of Jewish writers who chose to reclaim the German language as their own after 1945.

No Prerequisites Taught in English 3 Credit Hours FC-AESTH / FC-POWER

What You Will Study

  • The German-Jewish dialogue from the Enlightenment to the present day
  • Literary, philosophical, and autobiographical texts by German-Jewish writers
  • Questions of belonging, difference, and identity through language
  • The concept of minor literature (Deleuze & Guattari)
  • Language and violence under the Third Reich (Klemperer)
  • Poetry after Auschwitz — Celan's Todesfuge and Nelly Sachs
  • Writing history after the Holocaust
  • Jewish writers reclaiming German after 1945
  • The power of multilingualism in contemporary fiction
  • Connections between historical minority experience and today
